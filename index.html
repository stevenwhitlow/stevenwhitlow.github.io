    Grade of Membership model of occupational tasks

    Clustering Machine learning Data visualization Regression
    I build a Grade of Membership (GoM) model to group occupations based on the skills and tasks they require in day-to-day work. GoM models are an alternative to models like \(k\)-means clustering or PCA and produce "soft" unsupervised groupings in which occupations can be considered as partial members of multiple groups. Using three data sources, I show that these groupings strongly predict changes in wages earned by workers after they switch occupations.

    Immigration and worker location choice

    Regression Causal inference Data visualization
    I examine patterns of immigration and the resulting effect on the location choices of workers who were born in the US, using regression analysis to study the impact of immigration at the state level while correcting for selection into states using a two-step semiparametric method.

    I find that correcting for selection is important for causal inference in this context, increasing the estimated wage elasticity of immigration at least 30 percent. This is driven by high-ability workers tending to locate in states with high levels of immigration. This illustrates the potential importance of selection in causal inference when using nonrandomized data.

    Equalization payments and labour market adjustment

    Time series analysis
    We examine the effect that Canada’s equalization system on short-term patterns of provincial labour market adjustment using a panel vector autoregression model. Net migration responses to asymmetric labour demand shocks appeared to be stronger in ”have-not” than ”have” provinces.

    Civil conflict and regional employment in the U.K.

    Regression Causal inference
    This project examines the effects of the Northern Ireland peace process on the labour market of that province. Econometric analysis using a regional-specific trend model suggests the existence of a substantial “peace dividend”.

    E.U. freedom of movement and the U.K. labour market

    Regression Causal inference
    I examine how immigrants into the UK vary in their skill distribution based on their EU citizenship status, resulting in differing wage effects on the UK-born workforce.
